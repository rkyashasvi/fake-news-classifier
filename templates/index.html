Introduction


Motivation

The goal of our project was to build a classifier that can identify whether a news article is fake or real, based on the text and title content of each article. We believe this task is important because misinformation and fake news play a huge role in influencing social media interactions and democratic processes, as seen by the extent to which fake news affected the discourse surrounding the 2016 presidential election. Being able to detect if a news article is ‘real’ or not would be extremely useful to many social media platforms, who could use these machine learning methods to suppress viral fake news articles circulating on their websites and thereby limit the flow of misinformation online.


Dataset

We found a dataset online containing 11,000 articles - specifically, the article’s title, text and a classification denoting whether the article is ‘real’ or ‘fake’. In this dataset, an article is defined as ‘real’ is one that has been written in a factual manner, and is consistent with ethical journalism practices; an article is defined as ‘fake’ if it is not ‘real’, including but not limited to satire, opinion pieces, and conspiracies.

The labels in this dataset were analyzed and produced by OpenSources, an organization that compiles professionally curated lists of unreliable or otherwise questionable sources. Each article has a varying set of word counts, varying from approximately 100 to 500 words on average.

Approach

Our approach involved trying various combinations of vectorizers and learners, primarily from scikit-learn, to see which yielded the best accuracy. We used a bag-of-words model, counting the frequency of each word in an article to determine if it is a feature for training a classifier. The three vectorizers we used for this were the Count Vectorizer, TF-IDF Vectorizer, and Hashing Vectorizer; the four learners we used were Multinomial Bayes, Passive Aggressive Classifiers, Logistic Regression and Nearest Neighbor. Additionally, we also later performed an Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to determine which words were most associated with 'real' and 'fake' news articles.

Results

Vectorizer Classifier Accuracy
Tf-idf ZeroR 50.1%
Count Multinomial NB 85.9%
Tf-idf Multinomial NB 85.9%
Tf-idf Passive Aggressive 93.3%
Tf-idf Passive Aggressive + Bigrams 93.9%
Tf-idf Logistic Regression 91.1%
Tf-idf KNN 54.9%
Hashing Multinomial NB 90.1%

As can be seen above, we found that the combination of a TF-IDF Vectorizer and a Passive Aggressive Classifier with bigrams yielded the highest accuracy of 93.9%.

Additionally, our tf-idf and Passive Aggressive Classifier provided some other interesting results: the top three words associated with fake articles were ‘October’, ‘Hillary’ and ‘share’, while the top three words associated with real articles were ‘said’, ‘says’ and ‘Fridays’.
